🔬 The Double Slit Experiment

The double-slit experiment is one of the most famous demonstrations of wave-particle duality. When particles (photons, electrons, or atoms) are fired one at a time through two narrow slits, they create an interference pattern - something only waves should do. This suggests that each particle passes through both slits simultaneously as a wave, then interferes with itself before being detected at a single point.

If you add a detector to determine which slit a particle goes through, the interference pattern disappears! This is the essence of quantum measurement - observation affects the system.

📐 Key Formulas

Fringe Spacing (Young's Equation)
Δy = λL / d
Path Difference for Maxima
d sin(θ) = mλ
Path Difference for Minima
d sin(θ) = (m + ½)λ
Double-Slit Intensity
I(θ) = I₀ cos²(πd sinθ/λ)

🔣 Variable Definitions

λ = Wavelength of light/particle
d = Distance between slits
L = Distance from slits to screen
θ = Angle from center to fringe
m = Order number (0, 1, 2, ...)
Δy = Distance between adjacent fringes

📊 Interference Diagram

Wavefronts from both slits interfere constructively (bright fringes) where path difference = mλ, and destructively (dark fringes) where path difference = (m+½)λ.

💡 Key Insights

  • Fringe spacing increases with longer wavelength and decreases with larger slit separation
  • Single-slit diffraction creates an envelope that dims outer fringes
  • Which-path detection destroys interference - the act of measurement matters
  • de Broglie wavelength: λ = h/p applies to electrons and neutrons
  • The same physics explains both optical diffraction and quantum particle behavior

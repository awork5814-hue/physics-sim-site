How Lasers Work

A laser (Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation) produces coherent, monochromatic light through three key processes:

  • Absorption: Atoms absorb photons and jump to excited states
  • Spontaneous Emission: Excited atoms randomly emit photons
  • Stimulated Emission: An incoming photon triggers excited atoms to emit identical photons (same phase, direction, wavelength)

Population inversion (more atoms in excited state than ground state) is required for lasing to occur.

Laser Cavity

The optical cavity consists of two mirrors:

  • High Reflector (HR): >99% reflective
  • Output Coupler: ~95-99% reflective, allows some light to escape as the laser beam

Photons bounce back and forth between mirrors, stimulating more emissions and creating amplification.

Einstein Coefficients
  • B₁₂: Absorption rate (ground → excited)
  • B₂₁: Stimulated emission rate (excited → ground)
  • A₂₁: Spontaneous emission rate

For lasing: B₂₁ = B₁₂, but we need N₂ > N₁ (population inversion) so stimulated emission dominates absorption.

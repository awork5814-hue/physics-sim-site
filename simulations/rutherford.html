📚 Theory

In 1909, Geiger and Marsden, under Rutherford's direction, fired alpha particles at thin gold foil. According to the prevailing "Plum Pudding" model, positive charge was spread throughout the atom, so alpha particles should pass through with only slight deflection.

The Surprise: A few particles deflected at large angles—and some bounced straight back! This led Rutherford to conclude that atoms have a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus.

🔬 Key Observations
  • Most α-particles pass straight through (atom is mostly empty space)
  • Some deflect at small angles (near misses)
  • Rare large-angle deflections (direct hits on nucleus)
  • Very rare backscatters (head-on collision with nucleus)
💡 Rutherford's Conclusion

"It was almost as incredible as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you."

The nucleus is incredibly small (< 1/10,000 of atomic radius) but contains nearly all the mass and all the positive charge.
